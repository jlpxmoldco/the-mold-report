# Mycotoxins and Reproductive Health: New Review Links OTA and Zearalenone to Fertility Risk

> Published: 2025-03-26 | Source: Journal of IVF-Worldwide | Author: Sajjad Y, Dib J, Soliman N, Alhmoudi M, Sajjad SG, Kandil H, Fakih M | Category: research

Canonical URL: https://themoldreport.org/a/2feb375dfaf0.html
Original source: https://jivfww.scholasticahq.com/article/132398-the-role-of-mycotoxins-in-reproductive-health-mechanisms-evidence-and-clinical-implications

---

A peer-reviewed review in the Journal of IVF-Worldwide synthesizes the evidence linking mycotoxin exposure to impaired reproductive health, focusing on ochratoxin A (OTA) and zearalenone (ZEA) as the two most extensively studied culprits. The authors — a group led by Yasmin Sajjad with collaborators from fertility centers in the UAE and UK — draw on epidemiological data, animal models, and experimental research to argue that mycotoxins should be on the differential when clinicians evaluate unexplained infertility, especially in regions where food-supply contamination is documented.

Mechanistically, the review describes ochratoxin A as a long-half-life toxin that inhibits protein synthesis, triggers apoptosis through loss of mitochondrial membrane potential, and causes DNA damage via adduct formation. In males, OTA exposure is associated with sperm abnormalities including decapitation, coiled tails, and enlarged heads, with downstream effects on motility and fertilization. Zearalenone, by contrast, is a nonsteroidal estrogenic compound that binds estrogen receptors and disrupts endocrine signaling in both sexes, contributing to menstrual irregularities and reduced fertility.

Across the wider literature reviewed, the authors link mycotoxin exposure to reduced sperm quality, hormonal dysregulation, and elevated miscarriage risk, with proposed mechanisms including oxidative stress, epigenetic alteration, and direct toxicity to gametes and reproductive tissues. Their public-health takeaway is incremental rather than alarmist: dietary precautions, stronger food-safety enforcement, and reduced environmental exposure may protect reproductive outcomes, but the exposure thresholds that matter clinically remain undefined and warrant further study.

## Editor's Note

Reproductive complaints are common among patients with chronic biotoxin exposure but are often siloed from the rest of the workup. This review is useful because it frames mycotoxin-driven fertility effects as a mechanistic story — hormone receptor binding, mitochondrial injury, oxidative stress — rather than a vague association, which makes it easier to integrate into existing clinical reasoning.
